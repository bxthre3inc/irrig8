## PART 1: WHAT WE'RE ACTUALLY BUILDING [45-60 seconds]

*[Narrative — personal history, clear arc]*

"So Bxthre3 Inc is structured as a Delaware C-Corp holding company. Think of it like a venture studio. We have a core platform called AgentOS — that's the engine — and then multiple operating subsidiaries that run on top of it.

AgentOS is essentially an AI workforce orchestration system. I've been building this for two years. The key thing that makes it different: it's deterministic, not probabilistic. It doesn't use large language models for decision-making. It uses state machines, event cascades, and forensic audit trails. Meaning: it can't hallucinate, every decision is auditable, and it can run for forty-eight hours without an internet connection.

That last part matters because a lot of our customers — farmers, rural businesses — they don't have reliable connectivity.

The subsidiaries:
- Irrig8 — precision agriculture, water management, currently doing a two-field pilot with Colorado State University
- Valley Players Club — mobile gaming platform, already built and running, looking for venue partners
- Build-A-Biz LLC — business-in-a-box services for rural entrepreneurs
- And the newest one, which I'll get to: the SLV Technology Commons

---

## PART 2: THE SAN LUIS VALLEY ARBITRAGE [60-90 seconds]

*[Why Monte Vista? Why rural Colorado?]*

"I'm doing this in the San Luis Valley, specifically Monte Vista and the surrounding area. Most people ask why I'm not in Boulder or Denver. Three reasons:

First, land and power are cheap. We're looking at a forty-acre site for the campus that would cost five million in the Front Range. It's a fraction of that here.

Second, the problems are real. The San Luis Valley has serious water constraints. The aquifer is being adjudicated. Farmers actually face legal consequences for over-use. That creates a market for precision water management that doesn't exist in places where water is abundant.

Third, talent arbitrage. Remote work means engineers don't have to live in San Francisco anymore. But they also don't want to live in the middle of nowhere unless there's a community, infrastructure, a reason to be there. We're building that.

The vision is essentially: what if we created a Y-Combinator-style incubator, but focused on climate-tech and ag-tech, and physically located in a place where those technologies get tested against real constraints?

---

## PART 3: THE SLV TECHNOLOGY COMMONS — WHERE YOUR MONEY GOES [90-120 seconds]

*[Specifics, numbers, timeline]*

"So the SLV Technology Commons — SLVTC — is the physical infrastructure play. We're talking about three zones:

Zone A is the nonprofit 501c3 that owns the shared infrastructure. That includes an edge data center — solar-powered, containerized, hundred-kilowatt capacity — a co-working and makerspace with thirty stations, conference rooms, a shared lab for sensor calibration and soil samples, and eventually a demonstration farm.

Zone B is the incubator space where startups can locate. They get subsidized infrastructure — compute, connectivity, lab access — in exchange for locating in the valley and creating local employment.

Zone C is the manufacturing and assembly facility where we can build hardware locally.

Phase One costs one-point-two million. Breakdown: four hundred K for the data center and containers, three hundred fifty K for the co-working buildout, one hundred fifty K for the lab, three hundred K for solar and battery.

That's what this bridge round finances. Not salaries, not marketing — physical infrastructure that has residual value even if the software fails.

Timeline: permits filed in May, site prep June, containers delivered July, operational by September.

## PART 4: THE FINANCIAL SITUATION — HONEST VERSION [60-75 seconds]

*[Don't sugarcoat. This is the risk section.]*

"Let me be straight about where we are financially.

Cash on hand as of April first: one hundred thirty thousand, four hundred fifty dollars. Monthly burn is roughly thirty-five thousand — that's two engineers, cloud infrastructure, legal fees for the patents, and basic operations.

That gives us thirteen weeks of runway. If we don't close this bridge or land a major grant by July first, we halt operations. Not a soft landing — a hard stop.

The grant pipeline is real. We have three active SBIR applications — USDA, NSF, Department of Energy — totaling about nine hundred thousand. We also have an ARPA-E concept paper in for three million. If even one of those hits, runway extends into 2027.

But grants are uncertain. That's why we're doing this bridge.

## PART 5: WHAT COULD GO WRONG — RISKS [75-90 seconds]

*[Explicit risk disclosure — this builds trust]*

"I want to walk through the specific failure modes, not generic risk language.

First, the Water Court trial. Irrig8's pilot with CSU includes a June twenty-ninth hearing where we have to prove our forensic data is admissible as evidence. If the judge rules that our sensor data doesn't meet legal standards, that significantly damages the agriculture story. Not fatal, but a major setback.

Second, patent prior art. We're filing seven provisional patents by May fifteenth. If there's existing patents or publications we missed that cover the ten-point vector system or the forensic sealing mechanism, our IP moat is weaker than projected. We're doing searches, but it's never zero-risk.

Third, rural talent acquisition. We're betting we can recruit engineers to Monte Vista. Remote work helps, but it's still a constraint. If we can't hire, we can't execute.

Fourth, gaming regulation. VPC operates under sweepstakes law, which varies by state and could change. A regulatory crackdown would impact that revenue stream.

Fifth, plain old execution risk. I might mismanage the buildout, pick wrong contractors, or fail to deliver the CSU pilot. That's always a possibility.

I put the probability of total failure at roughly ten to fifteen percent. Not zero.

## PART 6: WHAT SUCCESS LOOKS LIKE — UPSIDE SCENARIOS [60-75 seconds]

*[What does winning look like?]*

"If this works, there are three likely paths:

One: Bxthre3 raises a Series A at a twenty-five to fifty million valuation within eighteen months. Ag-tech comparables — Semios, Farmers Edge — have all raised in that range with similar traction. Your SAFE converts, you get equity, and the Series A investor sets a price that values your early investment at five to ten X.

Two: One of the subsidiaries — probably Irrig8 or the Technology Commons itself — spins out and raises separately. You'd have pro-rata rights to participate in that round.

Three: Strategic acquisition. A major ag-tech player or a regional economic development fund acquires the portfolio. Could be an equipment manufacturer, could be a land fund, could even be a state entity.

The climate-tech and rural development angle also opens up non-dilutive capital — USDA, state of Colorado, impact investors — that doesn't dilute your equity.

## PART 7: YOUR SPECIFIC TERMS — WHY "DISPROPORTIONATE" [60-75 seconds]

*[The offer — clear numbers]*

"So here's what I'm offering you specifically.

Minimum investment is twenty-five hundred. You can go up to twenty-five thousand — beyond that we need to talk about accredited investor status and different structuring.

Standard pre-seed terms right now: ten million cap, twenty percent discount. For friends-and-family coming in early with real risk, that's not good enough.

Your terms: five million cap, thirty percent discount, plus pro-rata rights for the Series A. What that means in practice:

If you invest ten thousand, at a five million cap with thirty percent discount, you get equity as if you invested at a three-point-five million valuation. That's roughly double what a standard investor gets at this stage.

Plus, if we raise a Series A, you have the right — not obligation — to invest additional money to maintain your ownership percentage. That's how early investors avoid getting diluted down to nothing.

Also: first priority access to the Equipment Commons when it launches. We're building an AgEaaS — Agriculture Equipment as a Service — fleet. Subscribers get access to tractors, implements, precision ag tools on a monthly basis. You'd get that at cost.

*[Where the money goes — line by line]*

"If you invest, here's exactly where the money goes:

Thirty-five percent to patent legal fees. Seven provisional patents, five to six thousand each including attorney time. This is the IP moat — without it, we're just a services company.

Forty percent to SLVTC Phase One. Site preparation, permits, deposits on the containers and solar equipment. This is physical infrastructure with residual value.

Twenty percent to engineering. Keeping our two current engineers through July so we can hit the CSU pilot deadline and the patent filing deadlines. They're critical to execution.

Five percent to legal and administrative. LLC formations, trademark filings, compliance, the boring stuff that has to happen.

None of this is going to marketing, none is going to my salary, none is going to speculative R&D. It's patents, infrastructure, and execution.

## DELIVERY STYLE GUIDE

**DO:**
- Speak like you're explaining to a smart friend
- Pause after key numbers — let them land
- Vary pace: slow on risks, natural on narrative, clear on terms
- Allow yourself to go off-script if Joby asks questions (this is a conversation template, not a transcript)

**DON'T:**
- Read robotically — this is a voice memo, not a teleprompter
- Oversell — the "disproportionate" terms should feel like fair compensation for early risk, not a trick
- Apologize for the valley location — own it as an arbitrage
- Hide behind jargon — "deterministic state machines" is fine, but explain why it matters

**EMOTIONAL BEAT:**
- Confident on the tech (you've built it)
- Honest on the risks (you're not hiding anything)
- Excited on the vision (this is what you actually want to build)
- Flexible on the outcome (you value the friendship more than the check)
