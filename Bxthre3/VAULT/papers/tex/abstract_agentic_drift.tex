\begin.Abstract}
Multi-agent systems that spawn descendants recursively introduce a fundamental failure mode we term \emph{autonomous drift}: the progressive severance of descendant agents from human authority as spawning chains extend beyond the root operator's direct oversight. This failure is architectural, not behavioral---it results from the absence of a structural constraint ensuring that every agent, regardless of depth in a spawn tree, remains tethered to its originating human principal. We address this problem through a simple yet rigorous mechanism: immutable parent pointers encoded at spawn time that form an unbroken chain of custody from any agent back to the root human authority. Because these pointers cannot be altered, redirected, or excised after creation, drift becomes not merely unlikely but structurally impossible---an agent cannot claim provenance it does not possess, and no link in the chain can be silently removed without leaving a detectable discontinuity. We integrate this mechanism within the BX3 Framework, grounding immutable parent pointers in its existing accountability primitives so that spawn provenance, authority scope, and termination rights are preserved uniformly across all descendant generations. We conclude that recursive spawning without immutable parent lineage is an architectural liability in any deployable multi-agent system, and that the pattern should be treated as a required component of trustworthy agentic infrastructure.
\end.Abstract}
