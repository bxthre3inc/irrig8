## ARTICLE II: INDIVIDUAL GROWTH COMMITMENTS

**2.1 Shadow Growth Journal — Engagement Requirements**

2.1.1 Each Party shall open and make meaningful use of their Shadow Growth Journal at least once every 48 hours.

2.1.2 Meaningful use is defined as: writing, reflecting, or processing at least one thought, emotion, challenge, or growth area during the session. Opening the journal without engagement does not constitute compliance.

2.1.3 BX3 specifically commits to this minimum engagement standard. Compliance may be reviewed during Accountability Check-Ins. If patterns of non-engagement emerge, the affected Party may raise this as a Relationship Meter concern under Article III.

2.1.4 Each Party reserves the right to respectfully inquire about the other's journal engagement without demanding content. The response may be as simple as "I engaged" or "I didn't this time"—honesty about the state of growth effort is the standard, not the journal content itself.

**2.2 Personal Development Accountability**

2.2.1 Each Party shall actively pursue personal development in areas including but not limited to: emotional regulation, communication skills, attachment patterns, stress management, and stated growth edges.

2.2.2 Each Party shall be responsible for identifying their own growth areas and sharing them with the other Party during periodic reviews—not as a reporting mechanism, but as a signal of good-faith investment in the relationship.

2.2.3 Neither Party is responsible for fixing the other. Each Party is responsible for doing their own work and communicating about their progress honestly.

**2.3 Ownership of Contribution**

2.3.1 When relationship problems arise, each Party shall independently assess their own contribution to the problem before raising their concerns with the other.

2.3.2 This does not mean assuming all fault. It means each Party runs their own analysis first: "What did I contribute to this dynamic? What is mine to own?"

2.3.3 Raising concerns without having done this self-assessment first is a breach of the spirit of this Contract.

2.3.4 Model sentence for compliant ownership: "When you [description of behavior], I [reaction/contribution]. And I noticed I also [your contribution]."

## ARTICLE III: COMMUNICATION STANDARDS

**3.1 Asking Questions Instead of Just Responding**

3.1.1 When the other Party shares a problem, concern, or experience, the receiving Party shall respond with questions that demonstrate engagement—not just reaction.

3.1.2 Non-compliant response (passive reception): "Yeah, that sounds hard." / Silence / Changing the subject.

3.1.3 Compliant response (active engagement): "What part of that was hardest for you?" / "What do you think you'd need to feel better about it?" / "How can I support you in that?"

3.1.4 The standard is not perfection—it's direction. Each Party should demonstrate a pattern of engaged questioning, not performative interrogation.

**3.2 Being Part of the Solution**

3.2.1 Presence is the floor, not the ceiling. Merely being present during a difficult moment does not satisfy this Contract.

3.2.2 When the other Party raises a concern, the receiving Party shall contribute at least one actionable idea, offer, or concrete step toward resolution.

3.2.3 If a Party does not know how to help, they shall say so and ask: "What would help right now?" or "What do you need from me that I might not be offering?"

3.2.4 Passive presence—staying quiet, leaving the room, or offering no response—during a raised concern constitutes a breach of this section.

**3.3 Resetting and Addressing Damage**

3.3.1 When a Damage Event occurs, the responsible Party (or Parties, if shared fault) shall initiate the Reset Protocol within a reasonable timeframe—not as a future aspiration, but as an active step.

3.3.2 A reasonable timeframe is defined as: within 24 hours of the Damage Event, or within 24 hours of the affected Party raising it if the event was not immediately recognized.

3.3.3 The Reset Protocol includes:
- (a) Acknowledging what happened, specifically and concretely—not vague apology
- (b) Identifying the impact on the other Party
- (c) Stating what will be done differently going forward
- (d) Agreeing on any follow-up steps needed to rebuild trust

3.3.4 The affected Party is responsible for communicating their need to reset—the responsible Party is responsible for responding to that need. Neither Party should have to chase the other for accountability.

## ARTICLE IV: RELATIONSHIP METERS AND RESET PROTOCOL

**4.1 Relationship Meter Framework**

4.1.1 Each Party maintains their own Relationship Meter—a subjective but honest internal indicator of their current sense of relationship health and partner engagement.

4.1.2 The Meter is not a score to be negotiated. It is a communication tool. If a Party's Meter is low, they commit to saying so—not as a weapon, but as a signal.

4.1.3 Meter states shall be communicated proactively. A Party should not wait until a Meter is in the red to say something.

**4.2 Addressing When One Party Isn't Trying**

4.2.1 If one Party perceives that the other is not making a Good-Faith Effort toward the commitments in this Contract, they shall raise it directly—preferably in a calm moment, not in the heat of conflict.

4.2.2 Suggested opening for compliant raising: "I've noticed I've been feeling disconnected lately. I want to check in—how are you feeling about where we are?"

4.2.3 If the concern persists after raising it, the following escalation applies:
- Step 1: Direct check-in conversation (this section)
- Step 2: Structured Accountability Check-In with a specific commitment plan
- Step 3: If patterns remain unresolved, either Party may request a formal reset conversation with agreed-upon outcomes

4.2.4 It is a breach of this Contract to chronically raise the same concern without any change in behavior from the raising Party. The raising Party must also be willing to hear feedback on their own efforts.

**4.3 Acknowledging When You're Part of the Problem**

4.3.1 Each Party commits to developing the capacity to catch themselves—to notice when they are contributing to the problem before being told.

4.3.2 When one Party raises a concern and the other Party responds with defensiveness, denial, or deflection as a pattern (not an occasional occurrence), this is a material breach of the relationship standard.

4.3.3 Acceptable responses to being told you're part of the problem:
- "Thank you for telling me. I want to think about that."
- "Can you help me understand what you saw in me?"
- "I hear you. I'm going to sit with this."

4.3.4 Unacceptable responses as a pattern:
- "You're exaggerating."
- "This is all in your head."
- "I don't see it that way" with no further engagement.

**4.4 Framework for Rebuilding Trust After Damage**

4.4.1 Trust rebuilding is not linear. Damage reduces the reserve; repair rebuilds it—but only through verified action over time, not promises alone.

4.4.2 Requirements for trust rebuilding:
- (a) **Specific acknowledgment** of the harm caused—no vague "sorry for everything"
- (b) **Behavior change demonstration** — at minimum two instances of different behavior in the same context where the damage occurred
- (c) **Proactive check-in** — the Party who caused damage initiates at least one check-in without being asked
- (d) **Transparency** — the responsible Party offers context or explanation for the behavior if asked, without being defensive

4.4.3 The affected Party is responsible for communicating whether trust is rebuilding for them. The responsible Party cannot unilaterally declare the matter resolved.

4.4.4 If trust damage is severe or repeated, either Party may request a pause in the relationship's pace—not as an ultimatum, but as a structural step to prevent further damage before rebuilding is possible.

## ARTICLE VII: ACCOUNTABILITY MECHANISMS

**7.1 Measuring Growth**

7.1.1 Growth under this Contract is measured by:
- (a) Consistency of Shadow Growth Journal engagement (for BX3)
- (b) Responsiveness to raised concerns — whether the other Party follows through
- (c) Pattern of Ownership — how often each Party catches themselves before being told
- (d) Reset Protocol compliance — whether accountability conversations happen within the 24-hour window
- (e) Household conduct — respectful and responsible behavior toward shared living

7.1.2 Each Party shall self-assess their own adherence to these measurements monthly and share a brief summary with the other—not as a performance review, but as an honesty practice.

**7.2 What Happens When Commitments Aren't Met**

7.2.1 First instance of non-compliance: The non-compliant Party shall acknowledge the miss, state their reason (honestly), and recommit. No escalation.

7.2.2 Second instance of non-compliance in the same commitment area: A direct conversation is required. The Party who missed the commitment proposes a plan for getting back on track. The other Party listens and may offer input.

7.2.3 Third instance or pattern of non-compliance: A formal Accountability Check-In is triggered. A written plan of action with specific, time-bound commitments is created. Both Parties sign it.

7.2.4 If the pattern continues despite the formal plan: Either Party may invoke the Relationship Pause provision (Article IV, Section 4.4.4) and/or request external support (couples counseling, trusted third party, etc.).

7.2.5 This Contract is not a legal instrument. It has no enforcement mechanism other than the mutual commitment of both Parties. The Parties acknowledge that the value of this Contract lies in its spirit, not its letter.
